Will AI replace
our minds?

A short guide to working with a thinking machine.

Bartosz Naskręcki UAM/CCAI Warsaw, 8 June 2026
Opening. “I will not predict whether AI will take your job in 2042. We will try to answer a harder question: which parts of thinking are worth delegating, and which should we keep?” Ask the audience to remember their answer until the end.

These requests sound similar. Yet each one engages your brain differently.

Ask for a show of hands: first explanation, then doing the task, finally reviewing one's own work. Do not judge the answers. The point is that AI as tutor, contractor, and reviewer are three different roles.

Today's claim

AI probably will not replace
our minds.

But it will take over many activities that we used to perform ourselves.

So learn to direct it before you let it choose the route.
Distinguish the tool from responsibility. The calculator did not “replace mathematics,” but it changed which calculations we perform by hand. AI has a broader scope, so the change is more significant.

First, text breaks into pieces

The model does not see sentences as we do. It receives a sequence of tokens: words, word pieces, and symbols.

A token can be a word, a word piece, a symbol, or a fragment of code. This matters in mathematics: the model operates on representations of symbols and does not read their meaning in the same way a human does.
Step 2 · attention mechanism

Each token asks:
“Whom should I listen to?”

Alice told Maya that she had won.

Attention assigns different weights to earlier tokens. It does not solve the entire ambiguity, but it helps construct context.

Say: “Imagine a school party where everyone listens to everyone, but not equally.” Ask who “she” refers to. Language is ambiguous. Attention helps track relationships, but does not guarantee the correct interpretation.
For the brave

Attention in one formula

Attention(Q,K,V) = softmax(QKT / √dk) V
Qwhat am I looking for?
Kwhat do I match?
Vwhat do I pass on?
1. Compare the query with the keys 2. Turn scores into weights 3. Combine information using those weights

Vaswani et al., “Attention Is All You Need,” 2017.

Do not derive the formula. Give only the intuition: Q for query, K for key, V for value. Softmax turns comparison scores into percentage-like weights. Multiple attention heads can track different relations in parallel: syntax, references, code patterns, or dependencies between symbols.
Step 3

One layer is not enough

A transformer repeatedly combines and transforms contextual information.

The input consists of token and position representations. Later layers can encode relationships, roles, and procedures. There is no single drawer labelled “Pythagorean theorem.”

Knowledge is distributed; it does not live in one “fractions neuron.” Successive layers transform token representations. Think of a series of filters, each extracting relationships useful for predicting what comes next.
Where does this capability come from?

Two learning stages. Then tools.

I

Pretraining

The model learns to predict the next token from a vast number of examples.

Patterns learned from data are encoded in the parameters.
→
II

Post-training

The model learns to follow instructions, solve tasks, and respect rules more effectively.

It uses examples, evaluations, and automated verification.
→
III

Use: agent + tools

An agent reads files, runs code, checks results, and fixes errors.

This is not training; it is a way of using a trained model.

The exact data composition and complete training recipes of modern models are not public.

Separate three ideas. Pretraining builds general prediction skills and representations. Post-training shapes instruction following and task solving. An agent is not a third training stage; it adds an action loop and external tools. Do not claim that the model learns from every conversation in real time. Newer model processes are only partly described publicly.
Why are newer models so good at mathematics?

Because mathematics leaves traces

①

It has structure

Definitions, proofs, and code contain recurring rules and patterns.

②

It can be checked

Many results can be verified with a calculation, test, or program.

③

You can iterate

Proposal → test → correction. An error can become feedback.

④

It has tools

Python, computer algebra, and tests act as an external scratchpad.

The biggest change is not that AI knows more formulas. It can also use tools to check attempts.

This is the central slide. Distinguish remembering an answer from following a procedure that reaches it. Mathematics and programming often provide fast, unambiguous feedback. An agent can use a test, interpreter, or algebra system, but the tool must also be applied correctly. This does not eliminate errors, but makes them easier to detect than in questions of taste, meaning, or value.

Algebraic route

Argument using the difference

If the difference 1 − 0.999… were positive,

it would have to be smaller than 0.1, 0.01, 0.001, …

No such positive real number exists.

A model can offer five proofs. You choose the one that actually helps you understand.

Ask for the first reaction: true, or a trick of notation? Show two explanations. The second is deliberately less formal for younger students. For older students, mention limits or the geometric series. This illustrates how AI can provide different explanations of the same phenomenon.
Real mathematics · trap

A proof that 1 = 2. Where is the error?

Assume: a = b ≠ 0

a² = ab

a² − b² = ab − b²

(a − b)(a + b) = b(a − b)

a + b = b ← divide by a − b

2b = b, therefore 2 = 1

ALARM a − b = 0

Division by zero is disguised as ordinary cancellation.

INSTRUCTION FOR CODEX “Check every step. Identify the first error and the condition that was violated.”
1Reads the assumption:
a = b
→
2Computes:
a − b = 0
→
3Identifies the first error:
division by zero

Codex should not judge only the result. It should check every step and its conditions.

Do not reveal the alarm immediately. Give the audience a clue: which step requires a condition? This resembles an error found in mathematical hallucinations: locally fluent steps, one illegal move, and a globally absurd conclusion. Then show the Codex audit. A useful instruction is not “is this proof correct?” but “check every step, identify the first invalid one, and name the violated condition.” The assumption b≠0 is only needed for the final transition from 2b=b to 2=1. Codex should infer a−b=0 and reject the earlier cancellation.

The most important safeguard

Fluency ≠ truth

sounds confident

has been verified

A model can produce fluent and persuasive answers. It has no infallible alarm that activates when an answer is false.

“Sounding like an expert” and “being correct” are separate axes. In mathematics, ask for verification, a counterexample, a numerical test, or an independent proof. For factual claims, request sources and verify them outside the model.

This is the concrete advantage of an agent: it can create a file, run it, inspect failure, and patch the code. The program verifies the calculation; the formula explains why it is correct.
Short offline demonstration

When does Euler's famous polynomial fail?

n² + n + 41

For n = 0, 1, …, 39 it produces primes. 
for (let n = 0; ; n++) {
  const value = n*n + n + 41;
  if (!isPrime(value)) {
    return { n, value };
  }
}
$ testing the hypothesis… waiting to start

The experiment finds a counterexample. Mathematics explains why it appears.

Click the button. The result is n=40 and 1681=41². Ask whether this is accidental. For n=40, 40²+40+41=1681=41². The tool finds a counterexample, while a human or model notices the underlying relationship. A correct primality test can verify the finite list n=0,…,39, but does not explain why the first simple factorization occurs at n=40.

How to ask AI well

A good prompt defines the task clearly

GOALWhat do I want to achieve?
CONTEXTWhat do I know, and what have I tried?
CONSTRAINTSWhat level, scope, and format? What should AI not do?
DONE WHEN…What must be verified before I consider the task complete?
Instead of: “Do exercise 7.” Try: “I am in grade 8. Give me one hint. Then check my reasoning and identify the first error.”
These are also the four elements recommended in current Codex best practices: goal, context, constraints, and done-when criteria. At school, done should often mean “I can explain the method and solve a variant myself,” not “I have text to submit.”
Five rules of mental hygiene

Delegate the work. Keep the steering wheel.

  1. Try first.Write down at least a hypothesis, drawing, or question.
  2. Ask for criticism.Say “find the first error,” not “confirm that I am right.”
  3. Ask for verification.Ask for a calculation, counterexample, or second proof. Verify sources yourself.
  4. Protect data.Do not paste other people's data, passwords, or private conversations.
  5. Know how to turn AI off.If you cannot explain the result without it, it is not your knowledge yet.
Mention privacy and school rules. Do not moralize about cheating; show the practical cost. Submitting a finished answer skips training your own brain. AI can strengthen that training or help you avoid it.

The answer after 35 minutes

AI will not replace
our minds.

Those who can use it to think, verify, and create will retain more agency than someone who only presses “generate.”

Which part of thinking will you keep?

Appendix · audience questions

Three statements worth protecting

“Does the model understand?”

It depends on the definition. Performance on a task does not determine whether the model has human intuition, intentions, or experience of the world.

“Is AI creative?”

It generates new combinations and sometimes valuable solutions. The debate concerns novelty, intention, evaluation, and authorship.

“Can we trust it?”

Only within limits where we can verify the result and the cost of error is acceptable. A confident tone is not evidence.
